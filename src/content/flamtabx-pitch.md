# FlamTabX

*By Aditya Ratnaparkhi (Cohort 3); Venture Coach: Radwa Rostom*

A climate-adaptive, bio-based coating system designed to make homes cooler, healthier, and energy-efficient by reducing heat , controlling moisture, and preventing mold and microbial growth.

## 1) YOUR "WHY?"

I have lived in multiple regions across India, where extreme summer heat is a part of everyday life. Temperatures often rise beyond 45–50°C, and homes become enclosed spaces that trap heat, making indoor conditions uncomfortable and, at times, unbearable. During my time at university, I stayed in student dormitories without access to air conditioning. There were days when the indoor heat was so intense that it affected sleep, concentration, and overall well-being. It was difficult to rest, study, or function in such environments. At the same time, seasonal changes such as the monsoon brought another challenge dampness inside buildings. Walls would retain moisture, leading to musty odors and visible mold growth, making indoor spaces not only uncomfortable but also unhealthy. What struck me most was that while solutions like air conditioning exist, they are energy-intensive and unaffordable for a large part of the population. Millions of families continue to live in such conditions, facing extreme heat during summers and unhealthy indoor environments during humid seasons. As a chemical engineer working on materials and coatings, I began to see this not just as an inconvenience, but as a solvable problem. I realized that instead of relying only on energy-heavy cooling systems, we could rethink the building surface itself, transforming walls and roofs into passive systems that regulate heat, manage moisture, and prevent microbial growth. This led me to explore the idea of a climate-adaptive, bio-based coating system that can make homes cooler, healthier, and more energy-efficient. My goal is to develop a scalable solution that improves living conditions, especially for middle and lower income households who are most vulnerable to rising temperatures and limited access to cooling. I want to contribute to a future where safe and comfortable living is not a privilege, but a basic standard for every home.

## 2) PROBLEM

Extreme heat is rapidly emerging as one of the most critical climate, public health, and economic challenges in India. Summer temperatures are increasingly crossing 45–50°C, with several regions recording temperatures above 50°C and cities like Delhi exceeding 52°C in recent years. Heatwaves are becoming more frequent, intense, and prolonged, with 2024 recording 446 heatwave days across regions the highest since 2000 indicating a clear escalation in extreme heat events . More than 23 states, home to nearly one crore people, are now prone to frequent heatwaves, highlighting the widespread exposure across the country .The human cost of rising heat is severe. Heatwaves have become a silent disaster, with heat-related deaths exceeding 1,000 annually in several years, and over 12,000 deaths reported between 1951 and 2021. In 2024 alone, more than 41,000 heatstroke cases and over 140 deaths were reported, although actual numbers are likely higher due to underreporting . Globally, heat exposure is projected to cause 38,000 additional deaths annually by 2030 and over 100,000 per year by 2050 if adaptation measures are not implemented. While outdoor heat is extreme, indoor environments often become even more dangerous due to poor building design and heat-retaining materials. Studies in rural India show indoor temperatures reaching up to 40°C in tin-roof houses and averaging around 38.5°C in cement structures, with conditions remaining above 35°C for extended periods, including night-time . Unlike outdoor heat, indoor environments provide little relief, leading to prolonged exposure. As a result, nearly 80% of individuals report heat-related symptoms such as fatigue, dehydration, headaches, and muscle cramps, with severe cases including fainting and hallucinations . The burden of extreme heat is disproportionately borne by low- and middle-income populations. Inadequate housing, poor ventilation, and low-quality construction materials significantly increase vulnerability, particularly for daily wage workers, farmers, and urban informal populations . At the same time, access to air conditioning remains limited due to high costs, high electricity consumption, and unreliable power supply, leaving millions without effective cooling solutions.Beyond households, extreme heat is also impacting the broader economy and infrastructure systems. Rising temperatures reduce labour productivity in sectors such as construction, agriculture, and manufacturing, while increasing cooling demand and placing additional pressure on energy systems. It is estimated that by 2030, heat stress could result in a loss of 5.8% of total working hours in India, equivalent to nearly 34 million full-time jobs. Current responses remain largely reactive rather than preventive. Heat Action Plans focus on short-term measures such as water distribution and emergency response, while long-term building-level solutions remain limited. Additionally, seasonal humidity worsens indoor conditions, leading to damp walls, mold growth, and poor indoor air quality. Despite the scale and urgency of the crisis, there is currently no scalable, low-energy solution that can reduce indoor heat exposure while also improving indoor health. As climate change continues to intensify heatwaves, addressing indoor heat stress is not just a matter of comfort, but a critical challenge for public health, energy sustainability, and climate resilience affecting millions of households.

## 3) EXISTING AND FAILED SOLUTIONS

A range of solutions exists today to address heat and indoor comfort in buildings; however, they remain fragmented, short-term, and insufficient to address the combined challenges of heat, humidity, and indoor health. Cool roof and heat-reflective coatings offered by companies such as Asian Paints, Nerolac, and niche players like Excel Coatings and LuminX primarily focus on reflecting solar radiation to reduce roof surface temperature. While these coatings can lower surface temperatures, their impact on indoor thermal comfort is limited, as they do not address heat retention within building structures or night-time heat release, resulting in continued indoor discomfort. Thermal insulation solutions from companies such as Sika and Saint-Gobain aim to reduce heat transfer but are often expensive, require structural modifications, and are not suitable for retrofitting existing buildings, particularly in low-income and informal housing contexts.

Air conditioning systems from companies like Voltas, Daikin, and LG remain the most effective cooling solution; however, they are energy-intensive, expensive to operate, and inaccessible to a large segment of the population, while also increasing electricity demand and carbon emissions. Waterproofing and damp-proof coatings offered by players such as Dr. Fixit and Fosroc address moisture ingress but do not contribute to thermal regulation and, in some cases, can trap internal humidity, leading to damp conditions and microbial growth. Similarly, antimicrobial paints available in the market lose effectiveness over time, particularly in humid environments, and are not designed to perform under combined thermal and moisture stress. Emerging technologies such as radiative cooling materials demonstrate promising laboratory results, but they remain limited by cost, durability, and scalability challenges, restricting their real-world adoption.

Across residential, industrial, and commercial settings including homes, warehouses, factories, and public infrastructure these solutions address only a single dimension of the problem, either heat, moisture, or microbial growth, resulting in incomplete and often short-lived outcomes. As a result, buildings may remain thermally uncomfortable, energy-intensive, or unhealthy despite multiple interventions. This fragmented approach highlights a critical gap: there is currently no scalable, affordable, and passive solution that can simultaneously reduce indoor heat, manage moisture, and improve indoor environmental health. This creates a clear opportunity to develop a climate-adaptive, material-based solution capable of maintaining comfortable indoor conditions across homes and industrial spaces without increasing energy consumption.

## 4) TARGET AUDIENCE & CUSTOMER DEFINITION

The primary target segment for this solution is residential households in hot and humid regions, particularly middle- and lower-middle-income families living in independent houses, low-rise buildings, and informal housing where indoor temperatures often exceed 45–50°C. A key challenge is the lack of access to affordable cooling: currently, only around 8% of Indian households own air conditioning units, leaving the vast majority of the population exposed to extreme heat conditions . At the same time, rising temperatures are significantly increasing cooling demand, which is projected to grow rapidly and drive a major share of future electricity consumption in buildings . Many households rely on fans or limited AC usage, leading to high electricity costs and insufficient cooling, while energy-poor households often avoid using cooling appliances altogether due to affordability concerns. With over 300 million households in India and a large proportion exposed to extreme heat, this segment represents a massive unmet need for passive, affordable cooling solutions that can improve indoor comfort and reduce energy consumption.

The secondary target segment includes micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) such as manufacturing units, warehouses, workshops, and commercial spaces where indoor heat directly impacts worker productivity and operational efficiency. Heat stress is already causing significant economic losses, with estimates suggesting that by 2030, India could lose up to 5.8% of total working hours equivalent to nearly 34 million jobs due to heat-related productivity decline . Rising temperatures are also increasing cooling demand across industrial and commercial sectors, adding pressure on energy systems and operational costs . MSMEs, which form the backbone of India’s economy, often operate in poorly ventilated, heat-intensive environments where indoor temperatures exceed 45–50°C, leading to worker fatigue, health risks, and reduced efficiency. This creates a strong demand for low-cost, scalable solutions that can improve working conditions and productivity without increasing energy consumption, making this segment highly relevant for early adoption and scale.

## 5) SOLUTION & VALUE

To address extreme indoor heat and unhealthy living conditions, we are developing a climate-adaptive, bio-based coating that can be applied on roofs and walls to make indoor spaces cooler, safer, and more comfortable without using additional energy. The coating combines multiple functions in one system. It reduces heat entering the building, helps maintain a more stable indoor temperature, and prevents dampness and microbial growth. This directly solves key user problems such as high indoor heat, poor sleep, rising electricity bills, and unhealthy indoor environments caused by mold and bacteria.

The coating works using a combination of advanced but practical material technologies. It includes heat-managing materials (phase change materials) that absorb excess heat during the day and release it slowly, helping to reduce temperature peaks. It uses reflective and UV-protective components to reduce heat absorption from sunlight and improve durability. A breathable, bio-based coating matrix allows moisture to escape, preventing dampness and mold formation. In addition, antimicrobial properties help reduce bacterial and fungal growth, improving indoor health. We are also exploring stimuli-responsive behavior, where the coating can adapt its performance based on temperature and humidity conditions. Together, this creates a passive climate control system that is low-cost, easy to apply, and scalable for homes, industries, and commercial buildings.

## 6) YOUR SPEED BUMPS

While developing a climate-adaptive coating for large-scale use, there are several key challenges that need to be addressed. A major constraint is affordability and scalability, as the solution must be cost-effective for middle- and lower-income households to enable widespread adoption. To address this, the focus is on using locally available and bio-based materials and designing a formulation that can be applied using existing painting processes without requiring specialized infrastructure. Another important challenge is market awareness and adoption, as most users are currently unaware of passive cooling solutions and rely on air conditioning or conventional paints. To overcome this, the approach is to demonstrate clear value through pilot installations, showing visible improvements in indoor comfort and potential energy savings, and building trust through real-world results. Additionally, access to early customers and distribution channels is a key barrier, which will be addressed by collaborating with local contractors, builders, and small businesses to test and deploy the solution. Initial progress includes developing early-stage formulations and exploring practical application methods, with a focus on moving from small- scale trials to real-world deployment and feedback.

## 7) EARLY ADOPTERS & MARKET VALIDATION

Early validation of the solution has been initiated through direct engagement with potential users across residential and industrial segments. Initial focus has been on MSMEs and small manufacturing units, where heat directly impacts worker comfort and productivity. I have visited multiple industrial units, presented early-stage prototypes, and gathered feedback on performance, cost expectations, and applicability. These interactions have confirmed a strong need for affordable, passive cooling solutions, with several users expressing willingness to adopt the product if it demonstrates measurable improvements in indoor conditions.

As a result of these engagements, two Letters of Intent (LOIs) have been secured from manufacturing units GreenCircle Chemicals (Mumbai) and Sargenic Chemical Pvt. Ltd. for pilot deployment and testing within their facilities. These partnerships provide an opportunity to validate the solution in real-world industrial environments, particularly in high-temperature conditions where productivity and worker comfort are key concerns. In parallel, discussions have also been initiated with gated residential societies, where there is growing interest in reducing indoor heat and electricity consumption. These early engagements indicate strong market interest across both residential and industrial segments and provide a clear pathway for pilot testing, validation, and initial adoption.

## 8) PROTOTYPE & DEMOS

Prototype development has progressed to early-stage validation through both lab-scale and real- world testing. Initial formulations of the climate-adaptive coating have been developed and applied on different substrates, including metal sheets and concrete panels, to evaluate performance under practical conditions. In industrial settings, the coating has been tested on external metal sheets, where a noticeable reduction in surface temperature was observed under direct heat exposure. In addition, comparative tests on coated and uncoated concrete panels have been conducted to understand thermal behavior and heat management performance.

Alongside thermal evaluation, multiple physical and performance tests have been carried out to assess the suitability of the coating for real-world applications. These include adhesion strength, scratch resistance, flexibility, drying time, gloss, water absorption, solvent resistance, and antimicrobial efficacy. The results from these tests have helped validate the durability, functionality, and application feasibility of the coating system. Feedback from initial demonstrations with MSMEs and industrial users has been positive, with interest in pilot deployments for further validation. The next phase focuses on scaling these tests into real-world pilot installations to measure temperature reduction, user comfort, and long-term performance under actual operating conditions.

## 10) GO-TO-MARKET STRATEGY

Our go-to-market strategy starts with a pilot-first approach, focusing on industries such as MSMEs, factories, and warehouses where heat directly affects worker comfort, productivity, and energy costs. In the first phase, we will implement pilot projects with early users who have already shown interest, and use these deployments to test performance in real conditions. The goal is to clearly demonstrate how the coating can reduce heat, improve comfort, and lower energy use. At the same time, we will closely work with customers to understand their needs and improve the product based on real feedback. This helps ensure that the solution is practical, effective, and ready for large- scale use.

In the next phase, the focus will be on scaling the solution based on learnings from these pilots. We will expand through repeat orders from existing customers and partnerships with contractors, industrial clusters, and facility managers. The aim is to make the solution easy to apply and adopt across multiple buildings. Key success measures will include number of pilot projects, customer

satisfaction, repeat usage, and performance improvement. Over time, the solution will also expand to residential buildings, enabling large-scale adoption. This step-by-step approach ensures that the product is continuously improved and scaled in a way that is practical, customer-driven, and sustainable.

## 11) KEY METRICS

The venture will track key metrics across impact, product performance, and business growth to guide decision-making and scale effectively. At an impact level, the focus will be on energy savings and carbon emission reduction. By reducing indoor heat and lowering dependence on air conditioning, each building has the potential to save approximately 500–1,000 kWh of electricity per year, translating to around 0.4–0.8 tons of CO₂ emissions reduced annually per building. At scale, deploying the solution across 10,000 buildings could result in 5–10 GWh of energy savings and 4,000–8,000 tons of CO₂ reduction per year, creating significant climate impact.

On the product side, key metrics include reduction in surface and indoor temperature compared to standard paints, durability over time, and overall performance under real-world conditions. These will be measured through pilot installations and field testing. On the business side, the focus will be on number of pilot deployments, conversion of pilots into paid customers, and repeat orders, which indicate product-market fit and scalability. These metrics will be regularly tracked and used to improve the product, refine the business model, and drive data-based decisions for growth.

## 12) INCOME STREAM(S)

## 16) ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACT

The venture is focused on reducing the environmental impact of buildings by providing a low- energy, scalable cooling solution that directly addresses rising temperatures and increasing energy demand. By reducing heat entering buildings, the coating can significantly lower the need for air conditioning, leading to estimated energy savings of 500–1,000 kWh per building per year. This translates to approximately 0.4–0.8 tons of CO₂ emissions reduced annually per building, contributing to climate mitigation. At scale, deployment across 10,000 buildings can result in 5– 10 GWh of energy savings and 4,000–8,000 tons of CO₂ reduction per year, demonstrating strong potential for large-scale environmental impact.

In addition to energy savings, the solution uses bio-based materials, reducing dependence on petroleum-based coatings and lowering the overall carbon footprint of the product. The coating is also designed for longer durability, reducing frequent repainting cycles and minimizing material waste over time. By improving indoor conditions without increasing energy consumption, the solution supports climate resilience and sustainable building practices. Key impact metrics such as energy savings, CO₂ reduction, and total area covered will be regularly monitored through pilot deployments and reported to stakeholders, ensuring transparency and continuous improvement. This aligns with the mission of creating scalable, sustainable, and energy-efficient solutions for climate adaptation in built environments.
